Introduction

Despite the fundamental truth that human rights are universal and inalienable, there are countless instances of their violation worldwide, whether it is workplace discrimination or violence against people during a conflict such as war. Civil Rights Lawyers are the soldiers of justice who set themselves on a mission to investigate such offences and ensure the restoration of human rights.

Also Known As:
  • Human Rights Lawyer
  • Civil Rights Attorney
  • Anti-Discrimination Lawyer
  • Human Rights Advocate
  • Civil Rights Solicitors

Typical Job Responsibilities

  • Fight for justice and equality for their clients by researching client cases, drafting legal reports, presenting the cases in court and negotiating settlements

  • Meet with clients and understand their issues, complaints and legal needs

  • Educate clients about their rights and inform them of the potential legal outcomes of their case; update them regularly about the status of their case

  • Research the case thoroughly; read relevant human rights laws, and examine similar cases

  • Liaise with paralegal staff to gather relevant information and evidence and analyse it

  • Draft and file appeals, motions and legal briefs and submit them in court along with other legal documents; carefully prepare physical evidence

  • Apply to court for emergency orders for promptly helping clients urgently in need

  • Present and argue the client’s case in court; record witness statements and all other case details

  • Help resolve the case, using relevant and effective legal strategies

  • Help clients negotiate financial compensation and other settlement terms

  • Rally public support and draw attention through demonstrations and other measures to highlight the injustices done to their clients

Standard Work Environment

Civil Rights Lawyers spend the better part of their workday in their office, reading, researching, documenting findings, and drafting legal documents. You may research by sifting through computer databases, browsing the internet, or perusing books from a library. During trials, Civil Rights Lawyers must be present at court.

A Civil Rights Lawyer must occasionally travel to meet with clients at various locations, such as homes, hospitals, or prisons, to collect evidence or facts that will help them guard the human rights of their clients. Some Civil Rights Attorneys may travel to appear in court as and when required.

Work Schedule

Civil Rights Lawyers typically work full time, although part-time or contractual work is also possible, even hourly. They put in at least 40 hours per week, constantly striving to meet case deadlines.

Employers

Most Civil Rights Lawyers work in private practice, either in a law firm or solo practice. They may also work as full-time permanent employees or on a contractual basis for a fixed number of cases or duration.
Finding a new job might seem challenging. Civil Rights Lawyers can boost their job search by asking their network for referrals, contacting companies directly, using job search platforms, going to job fairs, leveraging social media, and inquiring at staffing agencies. Check law journals and newspaper ads for civil rights law vacancies.

Civil Rights Attorneys are generally employed by:
  • Law Firms
  • Non-Governmental Organisations (NGOs)
  • Non-Profitable Organisations (NPOs)
  • Charities
  • Human Rights Organisations
  • Human Rights Courts
  • Media Houses
Workplace Challenges
  • Meeting the expectations of clients, some of which may be unrealistic with regard to time and desired outcomes
  • The need for excellent problem-solving, oratory and research drafting skills
  • Heavy pressure and long working hours to meet deadlines during trials; lack of work-life balance
  • The highly competitive job market
  • Finding a balance between the inadequate use of or over-dependence on technology
  • Adapting to the constantly evolving legal guidelines
  • Countering any negative perceptions that the public may hold
  • The need for patience, stamina and skilful negotiation to take a case to its rightful conclusion

Suggested Work Experience

  • Any academic program that a potential Civil Rights Lawyer undertakes typically requires a period of supervised experience, such as an internship or clinic. Law schools may mandate that you chalk up experiential coursework tackling human rights issues through the academic year.
  • You may find summer internships at law firms that take on human rights cases or at advocacy organisations that support the victims of human rights abuse . You may also seek volunteering opportunities at organisations that specialise in human rights. The careers department at your university is a valuable resource to guide you in the right direction.
  • Engaging with humanitarian extracurricular activities and externships during summer breaks under the supervision of experienced Civil Rights Lawyers is helpful. Such programs teach you practical skills in human rights law and help you network. They allow you to gain expertise in human rights law and network with people you may work alongside later.

Recommended Qualifications

Civil Rights Lawyers must earn their law degree, either an LLB (Bachelor of Laws) or a JD (Juris Doctor), depending on location. Either way, a bachelor’s degree is the first step to entering the profession. Law school entry is competitive and depends on multiple criteria, such as academic grades, standardised test scores, work experience, and interviews. Individual law schools mandate different admission criteria.

One pathway to becoming a Civil Rights Lawyer in some locations is to pursue an LLB program after graduating from high school and qualifying in the relevant aptitude test. The program teaches you the fundamentals of law and legal systems, preparing you for entry-level positions as Civil Rights Lawyers. Another alternative is to earn your bachelor’s degree in disciplines other than law and follow it up with a one-year graduate diploma in law or a professional examination. You would learn about the core components of the law and legal systems within a year. This pathway is informally known as law conversion.

Some Civil Rights Lawyers may choose to pursue further studies by acquiring a Master of Laws (LLM) degree, which enriches your academic knowledge of legal matters, including international human rights law. You may specialise in your particular interest and typically complete an internship as well. You may also opt for a Master of Arts (MA) degree in Human Rights , in which you examine human rights within the framework of anthropology, international relations, law, and political science.

Certifications, Licenses and Registration

Certification demonstrates a Civil Rights Lawyer’s competence in a skill set, typically through work experience, training, and passing an examination. When acquired from an objective and reputed organisation, it can help you stand out in a competitive job market, carry a significant salary premium of up to 18 per cent, increase your chances of advancement, and allow you to become an independent consultant. Successful certification programs protect public welfare by incorporating a Code of Ethics.

Individual government entities conduct the licensing process. It typically requires the passage of an examination in addition to the fulfilment of eligibility requirements, such as a minimum level of education, work experience, training, or the completion of an internship, residency, or apprenticeship.

Depending on location, Civil Rights Lawyers take licensing examinations called bar examinations, typically after graduating from law school. The bar test is mandatory to practise as a lawyer in any jurisdiction and typically requires extensive preparation alongside academic commitments. On passing the bar test, you are admitted to the bar in that jurisdiction and become eligible to work as a Civil Rights Lawyer.

Projected Career Map

Performance, experience, and the acquisition of professional qualifications drive career progression. Employees with consistently high levels of performance may be eligible for promotion every two to three years.

In private practice, a Civil Rights Lawyer’s performance may be measured in whether the targeted amount of work chargeable to clients has been achieved . Becoming the Head of Department in the enterprise would make you responsible for departmental staff and profits.

Starting as an associate, you would typically work in a team under senior colleagues. Civil Rights Lawyers assume more significant and independent responsibilities as they acquire experience and sharpen their technical legal skills, client-handling abilities and business development know-how. Senior roles would require you to supervise junior colleagues.

Beneficial Professional Development

Continuing professional development (CPD) will help an active Civil Rights Lawyer build personal skills and proficiency through work-based learning, a professional activity, formal education, or self-directed learning. It allows you to upskill continually, regardless of your age, job, or level of knowledge.

While specific courses and requirements may vary by location, Civil Rights Lawyers must typically engage with continuing legal education programs periodically. They may pursue either in-person or online courses in legal ethics, taxes & tax fraud, and legal matters related to healthcare. You may find that your law school or bar association offers you courses to keep current with the latest legislative developments that may impact your practice. Keep in mind that business administration courses can sharpen your business acumen as well as enhance your marketability.


Conclusion

If you are passionate about civil rights, fairness and justice for all, being a Civil Rights Attorney would not only fulfil your ambitions and dreams but serve to protect and fulfil those of many others. Despite the numerous challenges of the career, it is rewarding to know that you are equipped with the knowledge, skills and experience to safeguard the rights of vulnerable civilians.

Advice from the Wise

Build thorough knowledge of how to file a legal appeal and present a case to a judge. Meticulously investigate the legal data. Be skilled in formulating legal briefs and negotiating settlements on legal terms. Remember that you will need to decipher and explain laws and rulings to your clients who may not be familiar with legal jargon.
